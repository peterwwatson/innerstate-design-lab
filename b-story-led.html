A walk through one session

Sit down. Here is exactly what is about to happen inside you.

You'll take about thirty full breaths, then hold empty, then recover — six times over. This is the story of that half hour: what your body does, and where the practice is quietly trying to take you. Both, together, honestly told.

↓ Scroll to begin the session
01 Breathe · you settle in

The first thirty breaths change your chemistry — and your attention.

You begin breathing: full into the belly, then the chest, and you let the exhale simply drop. Thirty of them, easy and rhythmic. Within a minute your hands start to tingle. Nothing is wrong. Fast, full breaths are flushing carbon dioxide out of your blood, tilting it slightly alkaline — and that single change is what makes everything that follows possible.

Underneath the instruction to breathe is a quieter one, and it's the one that matters most in the long run: keep your mind on the breath, and don't get lost in thought. That's the practice beginning to turn inward.

The teaching's own map · lineage voice

Settle

The breath is not only chemistry — it's an anchor for attention. A body relaxed enough to let go, the teaching says, can store and settle in a way a tense, gripping body never can. Relaxing is treated as a skill, not a mood.

02 Hold · you release

You breathe out, stop — and the calm that arrives surprises everyone the first time.

After the last breath you exhale and simply hold, lungs empty. You expect to want air. You don't. Ten seconds, twenty, thirty pass with a strange, spacious stillness. Here's the honest part almost nobody is told: your oxygen is actually falling. The calm isn't stored air. It's that the urge to breathe is a carbon-dioxide alarm — and right now that alarm is switched off.

And into that stillness, thoughts come. This is where the practice reveals its real shape. The teaching says stress leaving the body is what generates those thoughts — so they aren't a failure of concentration. The moment you notice you've drifted and come back to the breath — that return is the entire practice. Not staying perfectly clear. Returning. Again and again.

The teaching's own map · lineage voice

Release

A learning and a purifying, one return at a time. Regaining awareness of being lost is the practice — the wandering was never the enemy. Every school of this shape trains exactly this move; only the vehicle changes.

03 Recover · you rest in stillness

When the urge honestly returns, you take one breath — and drop into quiet.

Slowly the CO₂ rebuilds and the pull to breathe comes back. That returning urge is your body's honest signal — the cue to recover. You take one full inhale and hold it about fifteen seconds, drawing your attention gently up toward your head, eyes soft and open. There may be pressure, warmth, a small rush. Then you let all of it go.

What to tell yourselfFeel the pressure, don't force it — keep the eyes soft, then let it all go.

What's left when you release is the point of the whole round: a settled, cleansed quiet with nothing to do and nowhere to be.

The teaching's own map · lineage voice

Stillness

This inward-facing calm is not a side effect of the breathing — in the teaching it's the doorway. Everything so far was travel; this is arrival, and the deeper stations of the practice open from here.

04 Rounds · you reach the bridge

Round after round it gets easier — and the practice finally names what it's for.

You do it again. And again — six rounds, each hold a little longer and, strangely, a little easier. Over weeks something larger shifts. At first every signal grabs you and you react. Later you wait for the urge and recover on it. Eventually the practice changes category entirely: you calmly execute a protocol, and holds of several minutes pass without strain. The same practice becomes a different thing as you become a different practitioner.

And here the teaching says the quiet part out loud. The purpose was never breath-holding. It's a bridge: turning inward to know and control what we are from inside, and to heal what blocks the way there — including remapping the old, hidden programming we run on without noticing.

A buried question · two answers, honestly tiered

How did Wim gain the ice capability?

The teaching's answer: the bridge, developed far — knowing and controlling the body from the inside.

The physiology's answer: under continuous monitoring his core temperature fell only 0.7 °C through 88 minutes buried in ice, no shivering, heat production roughly doubled (Bongers 2019). A documented feat — mechanism only partly explained.

Neither answer erases the other. Both are labelled for exactly what they are — and neither is a claim that you should try it.

05 Meditation · you return

The session ends the way every contemplative path ends: in a return.

After the final recovery there's no more breath to manage. You just sit in the settled state and notice it. This is meditation, and the move is the oldest one there is. Other schools say: return to the mantra, and follow it down toward transcendence. Same shape, different vehicle. Here the breath was the mantra, and the return was the whole practice all along.

⚠︎ Before you try this

  • Never in or near water — breath-holds cause shallow-water blackout; there are documented drownings, including practitioners.
  • Never driving or standing. Always seated or lying down; fainting can come without warning.
  • Tingling, light-headedness, hand cramping are common and harmless — ease off and breathe normally.
  • Don't force the hold; let the natural urge to breathe signal your recovery.
  • Skip or consult a professional if pregnant, or with epilepsy, heart conditions, high blood pressure, fainting history, or panic disorder.
  • A wellness practice, not medical treatment. "Healer / alchemist" is inspiration, not a cure.

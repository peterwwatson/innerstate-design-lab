Underneath "take thirty breaths" is the instruction that matters most over years: hold the mind on the breath, and don't get lost in thought. The inward turn starts here.

A body relaxed enough to let go can settle in a way a tense one can't — relaxing is treated as a skill, not a mood.

What you'll feel, and why it's fine

Tingling in hands and around the mouth is lowered CO₂ changing nerve excitability — strongest in your first weeks, and it fades as you get experienced. Light-headedness is expected; always be seated.

Into the stillness, thoughts come. The teaching reframes them: stress leaving the body generates those thoughts — so they aren't a failure.

Noticing you've drifted and returning to the breath — that return is the practice itself. A learning and a purifying, one return at a time. Every lineage of this shape trains the same move.

The honest part almost nobody is told

Your oxygen is actually falling during the hold — this is not "super-oxygenation." The calm is the quiet CO₂ alarm, not stored air. (Kox 2014 measured saturation dropping toward ~50% in retention.)

What to tell yourself in the hold

"Feel the pressure, don't force it — keep the eyes soft and open, then let it all go." The skill is to feel the right pressure, not to generate it. Keep eyes open (use a cover if it's strong).

Turn inward to know and control what we are from inside — and heal what blocks the way there, including remapping the old, hidden programming we run on without noticing. Round after round the hold gets easier as you stop fighting; the same practice changes category as you change.

The buried question — two answers, honestly tiered

How did Wim gain the ice capability?

The teaching's answer: the bridge, developed far.

The physiology's answer: under continuous monitoring his core temperature fell only 0.7 °C through 88 minutes buried in ice, no shivering, heat production roughly doubled (Bongers 2019) — a documented feat, mechanism only partly explained.

Neither answer erases the other. Both are labelled for what they are — and neither is a claim that you should try it.

How far to trust each part

The bold claims sit on a ladder of evidence, not a single yes-or-no — each rung labelled by tier. Nothing dismissed for being hard to measure; nothing sold as medicine.

Measured in trials
Fast breathing lowers CO₂ and mutes air hunger; practitioners blunted an inflammatory response (IL-6/TNF down ~50%). Oxygen falls in the hold — not raised.

⚠︎ Read before you practise

  • Never in or near water — breath-holds cause shallow-water blackout; documented drownings, including practitioners.
  • Never driving or standing. Always seated or lying down; fainting can come without warning and is common enough to expect.
  • Tingling, light-headedness, hand cramping (tetany) are common and harmless — ease off and breathe normally.
  • Don't force the hold; let the natural urge to breathe be your signal to recover.
  • Skip or consult a professional if pregnant, or with epilepsy, cardiovascular conditions, high blood pressure, a history of fainting, or panic disorder.
  • A wellness practice, not medical treatment. "Healer / alchemist / own 100% of your brain" is inspiration, not a cure.

What's real and repeatable is simpler than the legend.

Breathe, let go, hold, recover, and rest in a calm, open, inward-facing state you can return to anytime. The trials measure the shell; the teaching maps the inside; the owner reports from full intent — all held honestly, by tier, named and never sold as medicine. If you want to know how far it goes: don't take anyone's word. Test it in your own body.
